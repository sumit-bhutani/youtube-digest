## A. Executive Summary

### Core Thesis
A fifth-week U.S.-Iran military conflict has produced the single largest month of global equity value destruction on record (~$12 trillion wiped), with energy infrastructure disruption threatening a structural inflation shock. Markets are headline-driven, oscillating between panic and relief on fragmentary diplomatic signals. The underlying risks — Strait of Hormuz closure, Houthi Red Sea escalation, AI memory disruption, and stagflation — remain unresolved.

### 3–5 Primary Arguments

**1. The Strait of Hormuz closure is a structural economic event, not a temporary disruption.**
The Wall Street Journal reported (breaking during broadcast) that Trump was considering ending military operations without reopening the Strait. Analysts noted this would leave Iran in effective control of a waterway through which >20% of global oil export volume transits. Energy infrastructure repair timelines are estimated at months to years. This is not a "risk-off" trade; it is a potential permanent rerouting of global energy architecture.

**2. Iran is demonstrating precision strike capability that contradicts Pentagon messaging.**
Iran struck a U.S. airborne command-and-control jet and a Kuwaiti oil tanker 30 nautical miles northwest of Dubai within 48 hours — direct contradiction of Pentagon claims that Iranian military capability had been "obliterated." The tanker was laden with crude oil. This signal — precise targeting of commercial energy assets near UAE waters — is being interpreted as Iran demonstrating deterrence capability, not desperation.

**3. The diplomatic off-ramp is structurally weak and markets are likely mispricing resolution speed.**
BCA Research's Matt Gerken argued explicitly that the WSJ report may not be credible as a clean exit scenario. Iran's simultaneous tanker attack signals they are retaliating, not reciprocating diplomacy. The "wink and nod" scenario — tacit agreement where both sides stand down without formal deal — is the most plausible near-term path, but leaves Iran in control of the strait and unresolved nuclear enrichment issues.

**4. The economic burden distribution is deeply asymmetric — smaller Asian and South Asian economies face forced demand destruction.**
Societe Generale's chief economist stated that countries without fiscal capacity to absorb oil price increases (Pakistan, Bangladesh, Philippines) face mandatory demand destruction — not elasticity-based adjustment, but compulsory reduction in consumption. The Philippines' BSP projected inflation moving above 3.9% upper band. This is a distributional shock, not a symmetric global one.

**5. AI infrastructure investment remains resilient despite Google's TurboQuant memory-efficiency paper creating a rotational selloff in memory chips.**
Google's TurboQuant (released end-2025, publicized March 2026) claims 6x reduction in memory requirements for LLM inference without performance degradation. This triggered an overnight selloff in Micron and NAND flash-exposed names. However, Kai-Fu Lee (01.AI CEO) dismissed this as a "blip," arguing AI remains the most important technology revolution in human history. The real disruption is skill-mix displacement, not hardware elimination.
